# 1. Overview

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by persistent hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. It is one of the most common chronic diseases managed in primary care and is associated with significant long-term complications affecting the cardiovascular system, kidneys, nerves, and eyes.

The majority of diabetes cases in primary care involve **Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM)**, which is strongly associated with lifestyle factors and genetic predisposition. Effective management requires early diagnosis, ongoing monitoring, patient education, and coordinated care.

The goals of diabetes management in primary care include:

- achieving optimal glycemic control  
- preventing acute complications  
- reducing the risk of long-term microvascular and macrovascular complications  
- improving overall patient quality of life  

Management typically involves a combination of lifestyle modification, pharmacological therapy, and regular follow-up.

---

# 2. Diagnosis Criteria

Diabetes mellitus is diagnosed using one or more standardized laboratory tests.

A diagnosis can be made if any of the following criteria are met:

**Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG)**  
- ≥ 126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L) after at least 8 hours of fasting

**HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)**  
- ≥ 6.5%

**Random Plasma Glucose**  
- ≥ 200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) in patients with classic symptoms of hyperglycemia

**Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)**  
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L)

Unless the patient presents with clear symptoms of hyperglycemia, abnormal results should typically be confirmed with repeat testing on a separate day.

### Prediabetes

Patients with the following results are considered to have prediabetes:

- FPG: 100–125 mg/dL (5.6–6.9 mmol/L)  
- HbA1c: 5.7%–6.4%

These patients require lifestyle intervention and regular monitoring.

# 3. Risk Factors

Several factors increase the likelihood of developing Type 2 diabetes.

### Non-modifiable Risk Factors

- Increasing age (≥45 years)
- Family history of diabetes
- Genetic predisposition
- Certain ethnic backgrounds

### Modifiable Risk Factors

- Overweight or obesity
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Unhealthy dietary patterns
- Smoking
- Excessive alcohol intake

### Associated Medical Conditions

The risk of diabetes is higher in patients with:

- hypertension  
- dyslipidemia  
- polycystic ovary syndrome  
- history of gestational diabetes  
- cardiovascular disease  

Identifying these risk factors helps guide screening and preventive strategies.

---

# 4. Treatment Options

Diabetes management involves a combination of lifestyle intervention, pharmacological therapy, and ongoing monitoring.

Treatment goals typically include:

- achieving target HbA1c levels (usually <7% for most adults)  
- controlling cardiovascular risk factors  
- preventing complications  

Management plans should be individualized based on:

- patient age  
- comorbid conditions  
- risk of hypoglycemia  
- patient preferences  

### Lifestyle Intervention

Lifestyle modification is recommended for all patients and is particularly important in the early stages of Type 2 diabetes.

### Pharmacological Therapy

Medication therapy should be initiated when lifestyle measures alone are insufficient to achieve glycemic targets. Many patients will eventually require combination therapy to maintain adequate glucose control.

Regular monitoring of blood glucose and HbA1c levels is essential for evaluating treatment effectiveness.

---

# 5. First-line Medications

**Metformin** is generally recommended as the first-line pharmacological treatment for Type 2 diabetes unless contraindicated.

### Metformin

Benefits include:

- effective reduction of blood glucose levels  
- low risk of hypoglycemia  
- potential cardiovascular benefits  
- modest weight reduction or weight neutrality  

Common side effects may include gastrointestinal discomfort such as nausea or diarrhea. These effects can often be minimized by starting with a low dose and gradually increasing.

### Additional Medications

If glycemic targets are not achieved with metformin alone, additional medications may be added. These may include:

- SGLT2 inhibitors  
- GLP-1 receptor agonists  
- DPP-4 inhibitors  
- sulfonylureas  
- insulin therapy in selected cases  

The choice of therapy should consider patient-specific factors, including cardiovascular risk and kidney function.

---

# 6. Lifestyle Recommendations

Lifestyle modification is a key component of diabetes management and should be reinforced at every clinical visit.

### Healthy Diet

Patients should be encouraged to adopt a balanced diet that includes:

- increased intake of vegetables, fruits, and whole grains  
- lean protein sources  
- limited intake of refined carbohydrates and sugary beverages  
- reduced saturated fat consumption  

Portion control and consistent meal timing can also help stabilize blood glucose levels.

---

### Weight Management

Weight reduction is recommended for overweight or obese patients. Even modest weight loss (5–10% of body weight) can significantly improve glycemic control.

---

### Physical Activity

Patients should aim for at least:

**150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity per week**, such as:

- brisk walking  
- cycling  
- swimming  

Strength training exercises may also be beneficial.

---

### Smoking Cessation

Smoking increases the risk of cardiovascular complications and should be strongly discouraged.

---

# 7. When to Refer to Hospital

Referral to a specialist or hospital care may be required in certain situations.

### Severe Hyperglycemia

Patients with extremely elevated blood glucose levels or symptoms such as dehydration, confusion, or rapid breathing may require urgent evaluation.

---

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis or Hyperosmolar State

Patients with suspected diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) or hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state require immediate hospital treatment.

---

### Poor Glycemic Control

Referral should be considered when blood glucose remains poorly controlled despite multiple medications or when insulin therapy becomes necessary.

---

### Diabetes Complications

Patients should be referred for specialist care if complications develop, including:

- diabetic retinopathy  
- diabetic nephropathy  
- diabetic neuropathy  
- cardiovascular disease  

---

# Summary

Diabetes mellitus is a common chronic condition requiring long-term management in primary care. Early diagnosis, effective lifestyle intervention, and appropriate pharmacological therapy are essential for preventing complications.

Primary care physicians play a key role in providing continuous monitoring, patient education, and timely referral when more specialized care is needed.
