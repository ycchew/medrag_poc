# Dengue Management Guideline  
Clinic Group Clinical Practice Guideline  
Version: 1.0  
Last Updated: 2026  
Intended Audience: Primary Care Physicians  

---

# 1. Overview

Dengue is an acute viral infection caused by the dengue virus and transmitted to humans through the bite of infected *Aedes* mosquitoes, particularly *Aedes aegypti*. Dengue is common in tropical and subtropical regions and is a significant public health concern in many countries. Primary care physicians frequently serve as the first point of contact for patients presenting with suspected dengue infection.

Dengue infection can range from a mild febrile illness to severe forms such as dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome. Early recognition and appropriate management are essential to prevent complications and reduce morbidity.

The typical course of dengue infection consists of three phases:

- **Febrile phase** (high fever and systemic symptoms)
- **Critical phase** (risk of plasma leakage and complications)
- **Recovery phase**

Most patients recover with supportive care, but a small proportion may progress to severe dengue. Early identification of warning signs and careful monitoring are critical responsibilities in primary care settings.

---

# 2. Diagnosis Criteria

Dengue should be suspected in patients presenting with **acute febrile illness** who live in or have recently traveled to dengue-endemic areas.

### Common Clinical Features

Typical symptoms include:

- sudden onset of high fever
- severe headache
- retro-orbital pain
- muscle and joint pain
- nausea or vomiting
- skin rash
- fatigue

Some patients may also experience mild bleeding manifestations such as gum bleeding or nosebleeds.

### Laboratory Confirmation

Laboratory tests may assist in confirming the diagnosis:

- **NS1 antigen test** (early infection detection)
- **Dengue PCR test**
- **Dengue IgM and IgG antibodies**

Additional laboratory findings that may support diagnosis include:

- thrombocytopenia (low platelet count)
- leukopenia
- elevated hematocrit levels

Routine blood monitoring is recommended for suspected cases to detect early signs of complications.

---

# 3. Risk Factors

Several factors may increase the risk of acquiring dengue infection or developing severe disease.

### Environmental Risk Factors

- living in or traveling to dengue-endemic regions
- areas with high mosquito density
- inadequate mosquito control measures
- standing water around homes

### Individual Risk Factors

Certain individuals may be more vulnerable to severe dengue, including:

- elderly patients
- young children
- pregnant women
- patients with chronic medical conditions
- individuals with a previous dengue infection

Secondary infection with a different dengue virus serotype can increase the risk of severe disease due to immune-mediated mechanisms.

---

# 4. Treatment Options

There is **no specific antiviral treatment** for dengue infection. Management focuses primarily on supportive care and monitoring for complications.

### Outpatient Management

Patients with mild symptoms and no warning signs may be managed in outpatient settings. Management includes:

- adequate oral hydration
- fever management
- rest and observation

Patients should be advised to return immediately if warning signs develop.

### Monitoring

Regular monitoring is important during the critical phase, typically occurring between **days 3 to 7 of illness**.

Physicians should monitor:

- platelet count
- hematocrit levels
- hydration status
- vital signs

Patients should be reviewed frequently to detect early deterioration.

---

# 5. First-line Medications

Treatment for dengue focuses primarily on **symptomatic relief**.

### Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)

Paracetamol is the recommended medication for managing fever and pain in dengue patients.

Typical use includes:

- reducing fever
- relieving headaches and body aches

### Medications to Avoid

Certain medications should **not be used** because they increase the risk of bleeding.

These include:

- nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen
- aspirin
- other antiplatelet medications

Patients should be educated about avoiding self-medication with these drugs.

---

# 6. Lifestyle Recommendations

Patients diagnosed with dengue should follow supportive care measures to promote recovery and reduce the risk of complications.

### Adequate Hydration

Patients should maintain adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration. Recommended fluids include:

- water
- oral rehydration solutions
- clear soups
- fruit juices in moderation

### Rest

Adequate rest is important during the febrile and recovery phases of illness.

### Monitoring Symptoms

Patients and caregivers should be educated about warning signs and instructed to seek immediate medical care if symptoms worsen.

### Mosquito Prevention

Patients should take precautions to prevent further mosquito bites, including:

- using mosquito repellents
- sleeping under mosquito nets
- wearing protective clothing
- eliminating standing water near living areas

These measures help reduce the spread of dengue within the community.

---

# 7. When to Refer to Hospital

Referral to hospital care is necessary if patients develop warning signs or severe symptoms.

### Warning Signs

Patients should be urgently referred if any of the following symptoms occur:

- persistent vomiting
- severe abdominal pain
- bleeding from gums or nose
- blood in vomit or stool
- lethargy or restlessness
- rapid decline in platelet count
- sudden increase in hematocrit levels

### Severe Dengue

Immediate hospitalization is required for patients with suspected severe dengue, which may include:

- severe bleeding
- shock or hypotension
- organ impairment (liver, kidney, or central nervous system)

### High-risk Patients

Hospital referral may also be considered for high-risk groups, including:

- pregnant women
- elderly patients
- patients with chronic diseases
- patients unable to maintain adequate hydration

---

# Summary

Dengue is a common mosquito-borne viral infection that requires early recognition and careful monitoring in primary care. Most patients can be managed with supportive treatment, but clinicians must remain vigilant for warning signs that may indicate progression to severe disease.

Primary care physicians should focus on early diagnosis, patient education, adequate hydration, and timely referral for patients with complications or high-risk features.
