# 1. Overview

Triage is the process of prioritizing patients based on the urgency and severity of their medical condition. In primary care settings, triage protocols are essential for ensuring that patients who require immediate medical attention are identified quickly and managed appropriately, while patients with less urgent conditions are directed to routine consultation.

Effective triage improves patient safety, optimizes clinic workflow, and ensures appropriate use of medical resources. It is typically performed by trained nurses, medical assistants, or reception staff following standardized protocols. However, physicians should also be familiar with triage principles to supervise and support clinical decision-making.

The objectives of triage in a clinic environment include:

- identifying life-threatening conditions that require immediate intervention  
- prioritizing patients based on clinical urgency  
- directing patients to the appropriate level of care  
- reducing waiting times for critically ill patients  
- maintaining efficient clinic operations  

Triage may occur through different channels, including walk-in assessment, telephone consultations, or digital health platforms. Regardless of the method, consistent application of triage protocols helps maintain safe and effective patient care.

# 2. Protocols

Primary care triage protocols generally categorize patients into urgency levels based on symptoms, vital signs, and risk factors. Clinics may adopt a simplified triage classification system consisting of three main categories.

## Emergency (Immediate Attention)

Patients in this category require immediate medical intervention and should be referred to emergency services or hospital care without delay.

Examples of emergency symptoms include:

- severe chest pain suggestive of cardiac origin  
- severe shortness of breath  
- loss of consciousness or altered mental status  
- severe trauma or uncontrolled bleeding  
- seizures or convulsions  
- signs of stroke (facial drooping, limb weakness, speech difficulty)

When such symptoms are identified, staff should activate emergency response procedures and arrange urgent transfer to a hospital emergency department.

---

## Urgent (Same-Day Medical Assessment)

Patients with urgent conditions should be evaluated by a physician as soon as possible, typically on the same day.

Examples of urgent conditions include:

- high fever with systemic symptoms  
- severe abdominal pain  
- dehydration or persistent vomiting  
- worsening asthma symptoms  
- suspected dengue infection with warning signs  
- moderate injuries requiring medical assessment  

These patients should be prioritized ahead of routine appointments and monitored for any signs of deterioration.

---

## Non-Urgent (Routine Consultation)

Patients with stable or minor conditions can be scheduled for routine consultation according to clinic availability.

Examples include:

- mild respiratory infections  
- medication refills  
- chronic disease follow-up visits  
- minor skin conditions  
- non-severe musculoskeletal complaints  

Although these cases are less urgent, staff should remain alert for any symptoms that may suggest a more serious underlying condition.

---

## Triage Assessment Steps

A structured triage assessment may include the following steps:

1. **Initial patient complaint**
   - Identify the main symptom or reason for visit.

2. **Symptom severity assessment**
   - Determine the duration, intensity, and progression of symptoms.

3. **Vital signs evaluation**
   - Measure temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation when necessary.

4. **Risk factor assessment**
   - Consider age, pregnancy status, chronic diseases, or recent travel history.

5. **Triage categorization**
   - Assign the patient to the appropriate urgency category and proceed with the relevant protocol.

Clear documentation of triage decisions should be maintained in the patient's medical record.

# 3. FAQ

## What should staff do if they are unsure about the urgency of a case?

When uncertainty exists, the safest approach is to **escalate the case to a physician or senior clinical staff member**. Patient safety should always take priority over workflow efficiency.

---

## Can triage be performed over the phone?

Yes. Telephone triage can be useful for assessing symptoms before the patient arrives at the clinic. Staff should follow standardized questioning protocols and advise patients to seek emergency care if severe symptoms are suspected.

---

## How often should triage protocols be reviewed?

Triage protocols should be reviewed periodically to ensure alignment with current clinical guidelines and clinic operations. Updates may be required when new public health concerns or clinical recommendations arise.

---

## Should all patients have their vital signs checked during triage?

Vital signs are recommended for patients presenting with moderate to severe symptoms or when clinical assessment suggests possible instability. For routine visits, vital signs may be measured during the physician consultation instead.

---

## What documentation is required for triage?

The triage process should include documentation of:

- presenting complaint  
- relevant symptoms  
- vital signs (if measured)  
- triage category assigned  
- actions taken or referrals made  

Proper documentation ensures accountability and supports continuity of care.

---

# Summary

Triage protocols are essential for ensuring safe and efficient patient management in primary care clinics. By systematically assessing symptoms and prioritizing patients based on urgency, clinics can provide timely care to those who need it most while maintaining effective workflow for routine consultations.

All clinic staff involved in triage should receive appropriate training and follow standardized procedures to support consistent and safe patient assessment.
