# 1. Overview

Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is a common chronic condition characterized by the reflux of gastric contents into the esophagus, leading to symptoms and potential complications. It occurs when the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) fails to function effectively, allowing stomach acid to flow back into the esophagus.

GERD is frequently encountered in primary care settings and can significantly impact patients’ quality of life. The most common symptoms include **heartburn** and **acid regurgitation**, though patients may also present with atypical symptoms such as chronic cough, hoarseness, or chest discomfort.

Most cases of GERD can be effectively managed in primary care through lifestyle modifications and pharmacological therapy. Early recognition and appropriate management are important to prevent complications such as esophagitis, strictures, or Barrett’s esophagus.

Primary care physicians play an important role in:

- identifying typical GERD symptoms  
- initiating first-line therapy  
- educating patients on lifestyle modifications  
- monitoring treatment response  
- identifying patients who require specialist referral  

---

# 2. Diagnosis Criteria

GERD is primarily diagnosed based on **clinical symptoms and patient history**. Diagnostic testing is usually not required for patients presenting with typical symptoms.

### Typical Symptoms

Common symptoms of GERD include:

- burning sensation in the chest (heartburn)  
- acid regurgitation  
- sour or bitter taste in the mouth  
- discomfort after meals  
- worsening symptoms when lying down or bending over  

Symptoms often occur after eating and may worsen at night.

### Empirical Treatment Trial

In many cases, a trial of **proton pump inhibitor (PPI) therapy** may be used to support the diagnosis. Improvement of symptoms after treatment supports a diagnosis of GERD.

### Diagnostic Tests

Further investigations may be considered if symptoms are atypical or persistent. These may include:

- upper gastrointestinal endoscopy  
- esophageal pH monitoring  
- esophageal manometry  

Testing may also be required to rule out alternative diagnoses such as peptic ulcer disease or cardiac causes of chest pain.

---

# 3. Risk Factors

Several factors can increase the risk of developing GERD.

### Lifestyle Factors

Common lifestyle-related risk factors include:

- obesity or overweight  
- high-fat diets  
- large meal portions  
- late-night eating  
- smoking  
- excessive alcohol consumption  

### Dietary Triggers

Certain foods may trigger or worsen reflux symptoms, including:

- spicy foods  
- fatty or fried foods  
- chocolate  
- caffeine  
- carbonated beverages  

### Medical Conditions

Other conditions associated with GERD include:

- hiatal hernia  
- pregnancy  
- delayed gastric emptying  
- certain medications that relax the lower esophageal sphincter  

Identifying and addressing these factors is an important part of effective management.

---

# 4. Treatment Options

Treatment for GERD typically includes a combination of **lifestyle modifications and pharmacological therapy**.

### Initial Management

Patients with mild or intermittent symptoms may respond well to lifestyle changes and over-the-counter medications.

### Pharmacological Therapy

Medication therapy is recommended for patients with frequent or persistent symptoms. The goals of treatment include:

- reducing gastric acid production  
- relieving symptoms  
- promoting healing of esophageal irritation  

Treatment duration may vary depending on symptom severity and response to therapy.

### Maintenance Therapy

Some patients may require ongoing maintenance therapy to prevent recurrence of symptoms, particularly those with chronic or severe GERD.

---

# 5. First-line Medications

### Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

Proton pump inhibitors are considered the **most effective first-line treatment** for GERD.

Common examples include:

- omeprazole  
- esomeprazole  
- pantoprazole  
- lansoprazole  

PPIs reduce stomach acid production and promote healing of the esophageal lining.

Treatment is often initiated for **4 to 8 weeks**, after which symptoms should be reassessed.

---

### H2-Receptor Antagonists

H2 blockers may be used for mild symptoms or as an alternative therapy.

Examples include:

- famotidine  
- cimetidine  

These medications reduce gastric acid production but are generally less effective than PPIs.

---

### Antacids

Antacids may provide **rapid short-term relief** for occasional symptoms but are not suitable for long-term management.

---

# 6. Lifestyle Recommendations

Lifestyle modification plays a key role in the management of GERD and should be recommended to all patients.

### Dietary Adjustments

Patients should be advised to:

- avoid foods that trigger reflux symptoms  
- reduce intake of fatty and spicy foods  
- limit caffeine and carbonated beverages  
- eat smaller, more frequent meals  

---

### Weight Management

Weight reduction is recommended for overweight or obese patients, as excess body weight increases abdominal pressure and promotes reflux.

---

### Meal Timing

Patients should avoid eating large meals close to bedtime. It is recommended to allow **at least 2–3 hours between the last meal and lying down**.

---

### Sleeping Position

Elevating the head of the bed may help reduce nighttime reflux symptoms.

---

### Smoking and Alcohol

Patients should be advised to stop smoking and limit alcohol consumption, as both can worsen reflux symptoms.

---

# 7. When to Refer to Hospital

Referral to a specialist or hospital evaluation may be necessary in certain situations.

### Alarm Symptoms

Immediate referral is required if patients present with alarm features such as:

- difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)  
- painful swallowing (odynophagia)  
- unexplained weight loss  
- persistent vomiting  
- gastrointestinal bleeding  
- anemia  

These symptoms may indicate more serious conditions.

---

### Persistent Symptoms

Referral should be considered if:

- symptoms persist despite appropriate PPI therapy  
- symptoms recur frequently after treatment  
- long-term medication is required  

---

### Suspected Complications

Patients with suspected complications such as esophagitis, strictures, or Barrett’s esophagus should be referred for specialist evaluation and endoscopic assessment.

---

# Summary

GERD is a common gastrointestinal condition frequently managed in primary care. Most patients respond well to lifestyle modifications and acid-suppressing medications. Early recognition and appropriate treatment can significantly improve patient symptoms and prevent complications.

Primary care physicians should focus on identifying typical symptoms, initiating first-line therapy, providing lifestyle counseling, and referring patients for further evaluation when alarm features or treatment failure occurs.
