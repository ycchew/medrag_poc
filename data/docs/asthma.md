# Asthma Management Guideline  
Clinic Group Clinical Practice Guideline  
Version: 1.0  
Last Updated: 2026  
Intended Audience: Primary Care Physicians  

---

# 1. Overview

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterized by variable respiratory symptoms and reversible airflow limitation. It is a common condition encountered in primary care and can affect individuals of all ages. Asthma symptoms often fluctuate over time and may be triggered by environmental exposures, infections, physical activity, or allergens.

Common symptoms include:

- wheezing  
- shortness of breath  
- chest tightness  
- cough, particularly at night or early morning  

The severity of asthma ranges from mild intermittent symptoms to persistent disease requiring long-term medication. Although asthma cannot be cured, appropriate management can effectively control symptoms, reduce exacerbations, and allow patients to maintain normal daily activities.

Primary care physicians play a central role in:

- early diagnosis  
- initiation and adjustment of treatment  
- patient education  
- long-term monitoring and prevention of exacerbations  

Effective asthma management requires a combination of pharmacological treatment, trigger avoidance, and regular follow-up.

---

# 2. Diagnosis Criteria

Asthma diagnosis is based on a combination of clinical history, physical examination, and objective assessment of lung function.

### Typical Symptoms

Patients may report:

- recurrent wheezing  
- intermittent shortness of breath  
- chest tightness  
- persistent or episodic cough  

Symptoms often vary in intensity and may worsen during the night, early morning, or after exposure to triggers.

### Lung Function Testing

Spirometry is recommended to confirm the diagnosis whenever possible.

Asthma is suggested by:

- reduced airflow (decreased FEV1/FVC ratio)  
- significant improvement in lung function after bronchodilator administration  

A **reversibility test** showing an increase in FEV1 of ≥12% and ≥200 mL after bronchodilator use supports the diagnosis.

### Additional Tests

Additional evaluations may include:

- peak expiratory flow (PEF) monitoring  
- allergy testing  
- chest imaging when alternative diagnoses are suspected  

Diagnosis should also involve ruling out other respiratory conditions such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) or respiratory infections.

---

# 3. Risk Factors

Several factors increase the likelihood of developing asthma or worsening asthma symptoms.

### Genetic and Biological Factors

- family history of asthma or allergic conditions  
- personal history of allergies or eczema  
- childhood respiratory infections  

### Environmental Factors

Exposure to environmental triggers may contribute to asthma development or exacerbation, including:

- airborne allergens (dust mites, pollen, mold)  
- air pollution  
- tobacco smoke  
- occupational irritants or chemicals  

### Lifestyle and Health Factors

Additional risk factors may include:

- obesity  
- sedentary lifestyle  
- stress and emotional triggers  

Identifying and minimizing exposure to triggers is an important component of asthma management.

---

# 4. Treatment Options

Asthma treatment aims to achieve and maintain long-term control of symptoms while minimizing the risk of exacerbations and medication side effects.

Treatment strategies typically include:

- controller medications for long-term inflammation management  
- reliever medications for acute symptom relief  

### Stepwise Treatment Approach

Asthma management is usually guided by a **stepwise approach**, where medication intensity is adjusted based on symptom severity and treatment response.

Patients should undergo periodic evaluation to determine whether therapy should be stepped up or stepped down.

### Monitoring and Follow-up

Regular follow-up visits are essential to:

- assess symptom control  
- review inhaler technique  
- evaluate medication adherence  
- identify potential triggers  

Peak flow monitoring may be useful for some patients to detect early deterioration.

---

# 5. First-line Medications

Pharmacological therapy for asthma generally involves both controller and reliever medications.

### Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)

Inhaled corticosteroids are considered the **first-line controller therapy** for persistent asthma.

Examples include:

- budesonide  
- fluticasone  
- beclomethasone  

Benefits include:

- reducing airway inflammation  
- decreasing frequency of asthma exacerbations  
- improving lung function

---

### Short-Acting Beta-Agonists (SABA)

Short-acting bronchodilators are used for rapid symptom relief during acute episodes.

Examples include:

- salbutamol (albuterol)  
- terbutaline  

These medications relax airway muscles and provide quick relief from bronchoconstriction.

---

### Additional Medications

For patients with uncontrolled symptoms, additional medications may be considered, such as:

- long-acting beta-agonists (LABA) in combination with inhaled corticosteroids  
- leukotriene receptor antagonists  
- biologic therapies for severe asthma

Treatment should be individualized according to patient response and disease severity.

---

# 6. Lifestyle Recommendations

Lifestyle management and trigger avoidance play an important role in asthma control.

### Avoidance of Triggers

Patients should identify and minimize exposure to known triggers such as:

- dust mites  
- pollen  
- pet dander  
- smoke and air pollution  

Environmental control measures in the home may reduce allergen exposure.

---

### Smoking Cessation

Smoking significantly worsens asthma symptoms and reduces treatment effectiveness. Patients who smoke should be strongly encouraged to quit.

---

### Physical Activity

Regular physical activity is encouraged for overall health. Patients should use appropriate preventive medication if exercise triggers symptoms.

---

### Patient Education

Patients should be educated on:

- recognizing early symptoms of asthma exacerbation  
- proper inhaler technique  
- adherence to prescribed medications  

An individualized asthma action plan may help patients manage their condition more effectively.

---

# 7. When to Refer to Hospital

Referral to hospital or specialist care is necessary in certain situations.

### Severe Asthma Exacerbation

Immediate hospital referral is required if patients experience:

- severe breathlessness  
- inability to speak in full sentences  
- cyanosis or oxygen desaturation  
- lack of response to initial bronchodilator therapy

---

### Poorly Controlled Asthma

Referral should be considered when:

- symptoms remain uncontrolled despite appropriate therapy  
- frequent exacerbations occur  
- high-dose medications are required

---

### Diagnostic Uncertainty

Patients should be referred for specialist evaluation when the diagnosis of asthma is unclear or when alternative respiratory diseases are suspected.

---

# Summary

Asthma is a common chronic respiratory condition that requires ongoing management in primary care. Early diagnosis, appropriate pharmacological treatment, and patient education are essential to achieving good disease control.

Primary care physicians should focus on controlling airway inflammation, preventing exacerbations, minimizing exposure to triggers, and ensuring timely referral when severe symptoms or complications arise.
