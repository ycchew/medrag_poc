# Hypertension Management Guideline  
Clinic Group Clinical Practice Guideline  
Version: 1.0  
Last Updated: 2026  
Intended Audience: Primary Care Physicians  

---

# 1. Overview

Hypertension is a chronic medical condition characterized by persistently elevated arterial blood pressure. It is one of the most common conditions encountered in primary care and represents a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, chronic kidney disease, and premature mortality.

Globally, hypertension affects a substantial proportion of the adult population and is often underdiagnosed or inadequately controlled. Many patients remain asymptomatic for years, which is why hypertension is often referred to as the “silent killer.” Early detection and effective management in primary care are essential for reducing long-term complications.

Primary care physicians play a critical role in:

- screening and early detection  
- risk assessment  
- initiating treatment  
- monitoring long-term blood pressure control  
- coordinating specialist referrals when required  

Management of hypertension involves a combination of lifestyle modification, pharmacological therapy, and regular follow-up to monitor treatment response and detect complications.

---

# 2. Diagnosis Criteria

Hypertension should be diagnosed based on repeated blood pressure measurements taken using standardized techniques.

A diagnosis of hypertension is typically made when:

- **Systolic blood pressure (SBP) ≥ 140 mmHg**, and/or  
- **Diastolic blood pressure (DBP) ≥ 90 mmHg**

These readings should be confirmed on **at least two separate clinical visits**, unless blood pressure is severely elevated or the patient presents with hypertensive emergencies.

### Blood Pressure Classification

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|---|---|---|
Normal | <120 | <80 |
Elevated | 120–129 | <80 |
Stage 1 Hypertension | 130–139 | 80–89 |
Stage 2 Hypertension | ≥140 | ≥90 |

### Measurement Best Practices

Accurate blood pressure measurement is essential.

Recommended technique:

- Patient seated quietly for **5 minutes**
- Back supported and feet flat on the floor
- Arm supported at heart level
- Appropriate cuff size used
- Avoid caffeine, smoking, or exercise within 30 minutes prior

Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring or home blood pressure monitoring may be used when:

- white coat hypertension is suspected  
- masked hypertension is suspected  
- blood pressure variability needs evaluation

# 3. Risk Factors

Multiple factors contribute to the development of hypertension. These include both non-modifiable and modifiable risk factors.

### Non-modifiable Risk Factors

- Increasing age
- Family history of hypertension
- Genetic predisposition
- Ethnicity

### Modifiable Risk Factors

- High dietary sodium intake
- Obesity or overweight
- Physical inactivity
- Excess alcohol consumption
- Smoking
- Chronic stress
- Poor dietary habits

### Associated Medical Conditions

Certain medical conditions increase the likelihood of hypertension or worsen blood pressure control, including:

- diabetes mellitus  
- chronic kidney disease  
- dyslipidemia  
- obstructive sleep apnea  

Identifying and addressing these factors is an important part of hypertension management.

# 4. Treatment Options

The management of hypertension typically involves a **stepwise approach** combining lifestyle interventions with pharmacological therapy.

Treatment decisions should consider:

- baseline blood pressure level  
- cardiovascular risk profile  
- presence of comorbid conditions  
- patient age  
- medication tolerance  

### Lifestyle Modification

Lifestyle changes are recommended for **all patients with elevated blood pressure**, regardless of whether medications are prescribed.

Lifestyle modification may be sufficient for patients with:

- mild hypertension  
- low cardiovascular risk  

### Pharmacological Therapy

Medication therapy should be considered when:

- blood pressure remains ≥140/90 mmHg despite lifestyle changes  
- patients have high cardiovascular risk  
- patients have diabetes, chronic kidney disease, or prior cardiovascular disease

Treatment may begin with **single-drug therapy**, but many patients eventually require **combination therapy** to achieve target blood pressure levels.

### Blood Pressure Targets

General treatment targets include:

- **<140/90 mmHg** for most adults  
- **<130/80 mmHg** for patients with high cardiovascular risk when tolerated

Targets should be individualized based on patient characteristics.

# 5. First-line Medications

Several classes of medications are considered appropriate first-line therapy for hypertension.

Common first-line agents include:

### ACE Inhibitors

Examples:
- Lisinopril
- Enalapril
- Ramipril

Benefits:
- effective blood pressure reduction  
- protective effects in patients with diabetes or kidney disease

Potential side effects:
- dry cough  
- hyperkalemia  
- angioedema (rare)

---

### Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs)

Examples:
- Losartan
- Valsartan
- Telmisartan

ARBs provide similar benefits to ACE inhibitors but are less likely to cause cough.

---

### Calcium Channel Blockers

Examples:
- Amlodipine
- Nifedipine

These medications help relax blood vessels and are particularly effective in certain populations, including elderly patients.

---

### Thiazide Diuretics

Examples:
- Hydrochlorothiazide
- Chlorthalidone

These medications reduce blood pressure by promoting sodium and water excretion.

---

Combination therapy may be considered when:

- blood pressure is significantly elevated  
- monotherapy does not achieve adequate control  

---

# 6. Lifestyle Recommendations

Lifestyle modification is a cornerstone of hypertension management and can significantly reduce blood pressure.

Primary care physicians should counsel patients on the following measures:

### Dietary Modification

Adopt a heart-healthy diet such as the DASH diet.

Recommendations include:

- increased intake of fruits and vegetables  
- whole grains and lean protein sources  
- reduced sodium intake (<2,300 mg/day)  
- limiting processed foods

---

### Weight Management

Weight reduction can significantly lower blood pressure.

Even modest weight loss can produce meaningful improvements in blood pressure control.

---

### Physical Activity

Patients should aim for:

- **at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise per week**

Examples include:

- brisk walking  
- cycling  
- swimming

---

### Alcohol Consumption

Patients should limit alcohol intake to:

- no more than **two drinks per day for men**
- no more than **one drink per day for women**

---

### Smoking Cessation

Smoking is a major cardiovascular risk factor and cessation should be strongly encouraged.

---

### Stress Management

Chronic stress may contribute to elevated blood pressure. Stress reduction techniques may include:

- mindfulness practices  
- relaxation techniques  
- adequate sleep

---

# 7. When to Refer to Hospital

Referral to a specialist or hospital evaluation may be necessary in certain situations.

### Hypertensive Emergency

Immediate referral is required when blood pressure is severely elevated with signs of acute organ damage, such as:

- chest pain
- neurological symptoms
- acute kidney injury
- visual disturbances
- severe headache

These situations require urgent hospital management.

---

### Resistant Hypertension

Referral may be considered when:

- blood pressure remains uncontrolled despite the use of **three or more antihypertensive medications**

---

### Suspected Secondary Hypertension

Patients should be evaluated for secondary causes when hypertension occurs in:

- young patients  
- patients with sudden onset hypertension  
- patients with resistant hypertension

Possible causes include:

- renal artery stenosis  
- endocrine disorders  
- obstructive sleep apnea  

---

### Complications

Patients with evidence of hypertension-related complications should be referred for specialist evaluation.

Examples include:

- heart failure  
- chronic kidney disease  
- hypertensive retinopathy  

---

# Summary

Hypertension is a common chronic condition requiring long-term management in primary care. Early detection, appropriate lifestyle interventions, and evidence-based pharmacological treatment are essential for preventing cardiovascular complications.

Primary care physicians should focus on:

- accurate diagnosis  
- risk factor modification  
- individualized treatment plans  
- regular monitoring  
- timely referral when complications arise
